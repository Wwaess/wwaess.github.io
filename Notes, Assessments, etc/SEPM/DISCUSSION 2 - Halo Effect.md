# The Halo Effect

```
Human emotions can affect the user experience, a fact which contributes to the complexity of user satisfaction with a product. Further complicating the process is the fact that user emotions on the first use of a product are likely to be different to their emotions once they become more experienced. 

Read Gu et al (2023) and discuss whether you agree with the authors that their findings can improve the accuracy of subjective evaluation.
```

This part explains that "evaluation has penetrated into all aspects of social life" to the extent that it's being thoroughly researched. To the extent that the verb used is "penetrated", this is probably a wolf in sheep's clothing - the concept means well, but could prove harmful. One method, called "*scale*", supposedly _scales_ well and is thus widely used - unfortunately I haven't found much information on what this actually involves. According to the paper, another proposed method by Daniel Kahneman (2004), the *Daily Reconstruction Method* suggests that subjects record the events of the previous day several times over a number of days and then rank the "pleasure obtained from different activities over a period of time". 

# The experiment
This paper aims to figure out when this "Halo Effect" diminishes depending on usability and visual aesthetic, tested separately; this was done by designing four fake websites: 
- two that had the same usability but **differing visual aesthetic**; 
- two that had the same visual aesthetic but **differing usability**.

They then split a cohort of 20 people into two groups, one for each pair of websites, and then asked them to: 
1. watch a video introduction about the website;
2. play around with the websites for 5 minutes;
3. learn what functions the websites had; and
4. without further instruction on the websites, use them to reach a pre-desired setting. 

All participants were then asked to evaluate the websites they used 6 times over one week - from first impressions of their actual usage of them. The paper's results conclude that the halo effect caused via visual aesthetics does wane over time, especially after actual website usage and noticeably by day 3; the halo effect caused via usability is comparatively more stable with no change over time. This would suggest that the evaluation of a website may be skewed by aesthetic designs **if conducted too early**. This already agrees with existing research, though this paper adds a temporal dimension to the halo effect. 


Personally I have several issues with the way this experiment was conducted. 

For one, the sample size is rather small and lacking in diversity - only 20 students from the same university, at around the same age (it's stated that "6 males and 6 females" were "between...20 and 30"), and in the same department of said university ("Mechanical Engineering"), and they all "had experience in using websites and all used computers to browse websites". Not only is that rather vague both in terms of what "ha[ving] experience" means and in how that conclusion was drawn, but also it may have introduced the bias that people who are familiar with website browsing would be more inclined to focus on certain characteristics of the websites being tested. 

For two, it's rather subjective. It's assumed for the visual-aesthetic test that a waning halo effect over time must imply that the rating is "more" accurate, but there's no objective ground truth, whatever that may be, to deduce what a "correct" subjective rating could possibly mean; certainly the paper makes no mention of this. 

For three, the time frame itself may have been rather short. The aesthetics test suggested that the halo effect _can_ wane over time; I would have hoped for the consideration that this could also be true about usability too, but that the rate at such waning could be smaller; again no such mention is in the paper.
